Every Capitol Audit entry assumes you already know what a "whip" does, what "cloture" means, and why the Rules Committee matters more than most committees with actual policy in their name. Most readers don't — nobody's ever laid it out in one place. This page is that place: who holds power in each chamber, how a bill actually becomes a law, and a plain-English, Black's Law Dictionary-backed glossary for every term of art this journal uses when it writes about Congress.

## The legislative branch, top to bottom

<div class="orgchart__chambers">

<div class="orgchart__chamber">
<div class="orgchart__chamber-head">
<h3 class="orgchart__chamber-title">House of Representatives</h3>
<p class="orgchart__chamber-meta">435 members &middot; 2-year terms</p>
</div>

<p class="orgchart__group-label">Leadership</p>

<a class="orgchart__box orgchart__box--lead" href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-house.html">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Speaker of the House <span class="orgchart__box-arrow">graded &rarr;</span></p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Presides over the House, controls what reaches the floor through the Rules Committee, and stands second in the presidential line of succession. Elected by the full chamber at the start of each Congress; can be removed mid-term by a "motion to vacate the chair."</p>
</a>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Majority &amp; Minority Leader</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">The majority leader sets the floor schedule and legislative agenda for the party in control; the minority leader organizes the opposition's strategy and messaging. Neither role is defined by the Constitution — both are creatures of party rules.</p>
</div>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Majority &amp; Minority Whip</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Counts votes before they happen and works to hold party members in line on close ones. The name comes from the fox-hunting term "whipper-in," the rider who keeps the hounds from straying off the pack.</p>
</div>

<p class="orgchart__group-label">The gatekeeper committee</p>

<a class="orgchart__box orgchart__box--lead" href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-house.html">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Rules Committee <span class="orgchart__box-arrow">graded &rarr;</span></p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Effectively an arm of the Speaker's office. Before almost any major bill reaches the floor, this committee writes the "rule" governing that debate — how long it runs, and whether any amendments are allowed at all. A "closed rule" means no amendments; that's how leadership controls outcomes without a single public vote on the substance.</p>
</a>

<p class="orgchart__group-label">Nonpartisan officers</p>

<a class="orgchart__box orgchart__box--lead" href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-parliamentarian.html">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Parliamentarian <span class="orgchart__box-arrow">graded &rarr;</span></p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Advises the presiding officer on House rules and precedent. Appointed by the Speaker, serves at will, not elected or confirmed by anyone.</p>
</a>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Clerk of the House</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Custodian of the House's official records, administers the floor when a new Congress convenes before a Speaker is elected, and certifies official vote counts.</p>
</div>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Sergeant at Arms</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">The House's chief law-enforcement and security officer, responsible for order on the floor and in the chamber's galleries. Can be directed to physically compel absent members to attend for a quorum.</p>
</div>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Chaplain</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Opens each day's session with a prayer and provides pastoral counsel to members and staff. A House officer since the First Congress in 1789.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="orgchart__chamber">
<div class="orgchart__chamber-head">
<h3 class="orgchart__chamber-title">Senate</h3>
<p class="orgchart__chamber-meta">100 members &middot; 6-year staggered terms</p>
</div>

<p class="orgchart__group-label">Leadership</p>

<a class="orgchart__box orgchart__box--lead" href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-senate.html">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">President of the Senate <span class="orgchart__box-arrow">graded &rarr;</span></p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">The Vice President of the United States, by constitutional assignment. Presides only occasionally and votes only to break a tie — the one point where the executive branch has a formal, if narrow, role inside the Senate chamber.</p>
</a>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">President Pro Tempore</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Presides when the Vice President isn't there — which is most of the time. By modern custom, the position goes to the most senior senator of the majority party. Third in the presidential line of succession.</p>
</div>

<a class="orgchart__box orgchart__box--lead" href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-senate.html">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Majority &amp; Minority Leader <span class="orgchart__box-arrow">graded &rarr;</span></p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">The majority leader is the Senate's most powerful officer by custom: sets the floor schedule, decides which bills get a vote, and controls the chamber's rules-based leverage — including the filibuster's practical limits. The minority leader runs opposition strategy and negotiates cloture agreements.</p>
</a>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Majority &amp; Minority Whip</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Same function as the House whips: counts votes, works the floor before a close one, reports the head count back to leadership.</p>
</div>

<p class="orgchart__group-label">Nonpartisan officers</p>

<a class="orgchart__box orgchart__box--lead" href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-parliamentarian.html">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Parliamentarian <span class="orgchart__box-arrow">graded &rarr;</span></p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Advises on Senate rules and precedent — most consequentially, rules on whether a reconciliation provision survives the Byrd Rule. Appointed by the Majority Leader, serves at will.</p>
</a>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Secretary of the Senate</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">The Senate's counterpart to the House Clerk: keeps official records, administers oaths, and certifies the chamber's official actions. The Office of the Senate Parliamentarian sits inside this office.</p>
</div>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Sergeant at Arms</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Chief security and law-enforcement officer of the Senate, responsible for order in the chamber and — like the House's — empowered to compel absent senators to appear for a quorum.</p>
</div>

<div class="orgchart__box">
<p class="orgchart__box-title">Chaplain</p>
<p class="orgchart__box-desc">Opens each session with prayer; a Senate office dating to 1789, the same year as the House's.</p>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</div>

## How a bill actually becomes a law

<div class="billflow" markdown="0">

<div class="billflow__step">
<span class="billflow__step-num">1</span>
<h3 class="billflow__step-title">Introduced</h3>
<p class="billflow__step-desc">Any member can introduce a bill. Revenue bills must start in the House — the Origination Clause.</p>
</div>

<div class="billflow__step">
<span class="billflow__step-num">2</span>
<h3 class="billflow__step-title">Committee</h3>
<p class="billflow__step-desc">Referred to the relevant standing committee. Most bills die here — never get a hearing or a vote.</p>
</div>

<div class="billflow__step">
<span class="billflow__step-num">3</span>
<h3 class="billflow__step-title">Floor debate</h3>
<p class="billflow__step-desc">In the House, the Rules Committee sets the terms first. In the Senate, any senator can filibuster — debate doesn't end until 60 votes invoke cloture.</p>
</div>

<div class="billflow__step">
<span class="billflow__step-num">4</span>
<h3 class="billflow__step-title">Passed one chamber</h3>
<p class="billflow__step-desc">A simple majority passes it. It then crosses to the other chamber and repeats steps 2 and 3 there.</p>
</div>

<div class="billflow__step">
<span class="billflow__step-num">5</span>
<h3 class="billflow__step-title">Reconciled</h3>
<p class="billflow__step-desc">If the two chambers pass different versions, a conference committee (or amendment exchange) hammers out one final text.</p>
</div>

<div class="billflow__step">
<span class="billflow__step-num">6</span>
<h3 class="billflow__step-title">President</h3>
<p class="billflow__step-desc">Signs it into law, vetoes it, or lets it become law without a signature after 10 days if Congress stays in session.</p>
</div>

<div class="billflow__step">
<span class="billflow__step-num">7</span>
<h3 class="billflow__step-title">Override, if vetoed</h3>
<p class="billflow__step-desc">A two-thirds vote in both chambers overrides a veto and enacts the bill anyway. This happens rarely.</p>
</div>

</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="advice-and-consent">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Advice and consent</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): the constitutional role of the Senate in approving or rejecting the President's treaties and appointments — treaties requiring a two-thirds vote, appointments a simple majority.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">Article II, Section 2 &middot; see <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-senate.html">The Capitol Audit: The Senate</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="blue-slip">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Blue-slip</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">A committee custom, not a written rule, letting a member (or, in the Senate, a home-state senator) formally object to a bill or nominee — historically printed on blue paper. In the House, it lets a member flag that a Senate-passed bill improperly originated revenue provisions. In the Senate Judiciary Committee, it lets a nominee's home-state senators block a hearing.</p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="byrd-rule">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Byrd Rule</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">A Senate rule barring provisions from a budget reconciliation bill if their effect is "merely incidental" to the budget rather than genuinely fiscal. Named for Sen. Robert Byrd. The Senate Parliamentarian advises on whether a given provision survives it — informally, whether it passes its "Byrd bath."</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">See <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-parliamentarian.html">The Capitol Audit: Parliamentarian Edition</a> and <a href="/articles/parliamentarian-byrd-rule-explainer.html">Field Notes: the Byrd Rule explained</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="censure">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Censure</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): an official reprimand or condemnation. A censure carries no removal from office and no loss of pay or committee seniority by itself — it is a formal statement of disapproval, short of expulsion.</p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="cloture">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Cloture</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">The Senate procedure for ending debate and forcing a vote, requiring 60 votes for most legislation under current rules (reduced to a simple majority for most nominations by rules changes in 2013 and 2017). Filing for cloture is the formal act that starts the clock on ending a filibuster.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">See <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-senate.html">The Capitol Audit: The Senate</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="conference-committee">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Conference committee</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">A temporary joint committee of House and Senate members convened to reconcile two different versions of the same bill into one final text before it goes to the President.</p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="expulsion">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Expulsion</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): the formal removal of a member from a legislative body. Each chamber of Congress can expel its own member by a two-thirds vote — the highest disciplinary bar the Constitution sets, used only 21 times combined across both chambers in American history.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">Article I, Section 5 &middot; see <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-senate.html">The Capitol Audit: The Senate</a> and <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-house.html">The Capitol Audit: The House</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="filibuster">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Filibuster</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Extended Senate debate used to delay or block a vote on a bill or nomination. Not written into the Constitution — it exists because Senate rules impose no general time limit on debate, and ending it requires a cloture vote most legislation can't reach without a 60-vote supermajority.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">See <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-senate.html">The Capitol Audit: The Senate</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="germane">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Germane</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): relevant, pertinent, closely connected to the subject at hand. House Rule XVI requires floor amendments to be germane to the bill they amend; the Senate generally imposes no such requirement outside limited contexts, which is why unrelated riders can attach to Senate bills but not House ones.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">See <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-house.html">The Capitol Audit: The House</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="gut-and-amend">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Gut and amend</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">The practice of taking a bill that has already passed one chamber, deleting its entire text, and substituting unrelated new text — while keeping the original bill number. Most often used by the Senate on a House-originated revenue bill: the number satisfies the Origination Clause on paper while the substance never actually started in the House.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">See <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-house.html">The Capitol Audit: The House</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="impeachment">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Impeachment</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): a formal accusation, by the lower house of a legislature, against a public official, of misconduct in office. Impeachment is only the charge — a simple House majority. Conviction and removal require a two-thirds Senate vote after a trial; impeachment alone does not remove anyone from office.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">Article I, Sections 2 &amp; 3 &middot; Article II, Section 4 &middot; see <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-house.html">The Capitol Audit: The House</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="nuclear-option">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Nuclear option</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">A maneuver that changes how a Senate rule is interpreted by majority vote on appeal of a ruling from the chair, rather than amending the rule's text (which would require 67 votes). Used in 2013 to lower most nomination cloture to a simple majority, in 2017 to do the same for Supreme Court nominations, and in September 2025 to allow en bloc confirmation of sub-cabinet nominees.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">See <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-senate.html">The Capitol Audit: The Senate</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="origination-clause">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Origination Clause</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">The constitutional requirement that all revenue-raising bills start in the House of Representatives, not the Senate. The Senate may amend such a bill freely once it arrives — the loophole that makes "gut and amend" possible.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">Article I, Section 7 &middot; see <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-house.html">The Capitol Audit: The House</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="point-of-order">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Point of order</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): a question about whether a pending proceeding is being conducted according to the applicable rules. In Congress, raising a point of order asks the presiding officer to rule on whether something on the floor — an amendment, a provision, a procedure — is actually allowed. The parliamentarian advises on the ruling; the presiding officer decides.</p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="quorum">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Quorum</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">Black's Law Dictionary (12th ed. 2024): the minimum number of members who must be present for a body to legally transact business. In both the House and Senate, a quorum is a simple majority of the chamber's total membership.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">Article I, Section 5</p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="reconciliation">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Reconciliation</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">A special budget process that lets certain fiscal legislation pass the Senate with a simple majority instead of the 60 votes ordinary bills need to survive a filibuster — subject to the Byrd Rule's limits on what qualifies.</p>
<p class="glossary__cite">See <a href="/articles/capitol-audit-the-parliamentarian.html">The Capitol Audit: Parliamentarian Edition</a></p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="rider">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Rider</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">A provision attached to a bill that has little or nothing to do with the bill's main subject, usually added because it would be unlikely to pass as a standalone measure. The House's germaneness rule blocks most riders on amendments; the Senate's comparative lack of one is why riders are more common there.</p>
</div>

<div class="glossary__entry" id="seniority">
<h3 class="glossary__term">Seniority</h3>
<p class="glossary__def">The informal custom of awarding committee chairs and preferred assignments based on length of continuous service, rather than any written rule. Senate Republican Conference rules explicitly state members are not bound by it when choosing a chair — it functions as a strong default, not a right.</p>
</div>

</div>

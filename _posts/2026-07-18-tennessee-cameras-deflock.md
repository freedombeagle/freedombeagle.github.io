Quick question. When did you vote for this?

As of this week, volunteers at **[DeFlock](https://deflock.org/)** — an open-source project that crowdsources the location of every license plate reader people can find — have mapped **at least 2,899 automated license plate readers in Tennessee**. Not traffic cameras. Not red-light cameras. Cameras that photograph *every plate that passes*, log where your car was and when, and pour it all into searchable databases.

And here's the part that should make you sit up: **2,497 of those cameras — 86% — belong to one private company.** Flock Safety. The same company [we covered last month](/freedom-beagle/articles/flock-cameras-fourth-amendment.html), whose network has been queried by federal agencies more than a million times through local police accounts, often without those departments' authorization.

The rest of the Tennessee map: Motorola Solutions runs about 135, Genetec 46, and a scattering of smaller vendors — Axon, Rekor, Skycop — make up the tail. And remember: that's just what volunteers have *found so far*. DeFlock has charted roughly half of Flock's national network. The real number on Tennessee poles is higher.

Nationwide? **116,943 license plate readers mapped in the United States** as of today, by DeFlock's own counter. That's not a pilot program. That's infrastructure.

## But here's the good news — people are fighting back, and winning

DeFlock also keeps a running scoreboard of communities that looked at the cameras and said no. It currently stands at **89 cities and counties** — contracts rejected, contracts canceled, cameras physically deactivated. Los Angeles canceled this month. Madison, Wisconsin shut its cameras off. Ypsilanti, Michigan banned ALPRs outright.

And one of those 89 is ours: **Knoxville canceled its Flock contract in February 2026.** A Tennessee city looked at the surveillance math and decided it didn't add up. If Knoxville can do it, so can yours.

Want to know how many cameras are watching *your* street? Open [DeFlock's map](https://deflock.org/), punch in your neighborhood, and count. Then maybe ask your city council the same question we opened with: when did anybody vote for this?

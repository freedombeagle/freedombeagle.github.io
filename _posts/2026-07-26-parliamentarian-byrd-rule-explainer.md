Quick question. Who's the Senate Parliamentarian?

If you don't know, you're not alone — and that's sort of the point. **Elizabeth MacDonough has run the Senate's rulebook since 2012.** She's never been on a ballot. Nobody in America voted for her. She was picked by one person, Majority Leader Harry Reid, and she can be fired by whoever holds that job next, for any reason, at any time.

## What the job actually is

The parliamentarian's day job is procedural referee: she advises the presiding officer on whether a motion is in order, keeps the chamber's book of precedent, and rules on points of order when senators raise them. Dry stuff, mostly. The part that actually shapes legislation is narrower — enforcing the **Byrd Rule** in budget reconciliation.

Reconciliation is the one path around the Senate's 60-vote filibuster: a simple majority can pass it. But only for provisions that are genuinely budgetary. The Byrd Rule lets any senator challenge a provision whose main effect is policy, not dollars, and the parliamentarian rules on whether it survives. Fail that test and the provision gets stripped — informally, it "failed its Byrd bath."

That single call has killed a $15 minimum wage hike (2021), three straight attempts at an immigration pathway to legal status (2021), and pieces of a 2026 reconciliation package Senate Republicans wanted, all on the same reasoning: the policy effect outweighs the budget effect.

## Where the office came from

It's not in the Constitution. Not close. The Senate parliamentarian traces back to Charles Watkins, an Assistant Secretary of the Senate who spent over a decade informally advising presiding officers before the office was formally created in 1935. The House's version took even longer to formalize — parliamentarian-type duties were split among officials called "messenger to the Speaker" and "clerk at the Speaker's table" until a parliamentarian was appointed every Congress starting in 1927, with the office itself only standing up as nonpartisan in 1977–79. Article I gives each chamber the power to "determine the Rules of its Proceedings" — that's the entire textual basis. Everything else is a century of custom.

## Why nobody's ever voted her out

Here's the part that should actually surprise you: **the Senate can overrule the parliamentarian by simple majority vote, any time, on any ruling — and it almost never does.**

Not once in 2021, across four separate rulings that killed Democratic priorities, did Senate Democrats — who had the votes — actually hold that floor vote to overrule MacDonough. Not once in the 2026 pressure campaign against her, with President Trump and multiple senators publicly calling for her head, has a removal or override vote reached the floor.

There's exactly one clean exception in fifty years: **Robert Dove, fired in May 2001** by Majority Leader Trent Lott, specifically over rulings against Republican-favored provisions. That's the only time a Senate majority leader has actually pulled the trigger on a sitting parliamentarian. Everyone else — nine people across both chambers since 1974 — left on their own terms, and every single officeholder tested by a change of party control got kept on by the other side. Both parties keep finding it easier to complain about a ruling than to actually vote it away.

## The Byrd Rule, in plain terms

Named for Sen. Robert Byrd, the rule exists because reconciliation was never supposed to be a backdoor for ordinary legislation — it's a budget tool, filibuster-proof for exactly that reason. The Byrd Rule is the fence around it: a provision survives only if its budgetary effect isn't "merely incidental" to a policy goal.

Why has it gotten so much more prevalent? Because the modern filibuster made reconciliation the only lane where a party with a bare majority can actually move anything. Cloture motions — the vote needed to break a filibuster — have exploded from 201 in the 2017–18 Congress to 336 in 2021–22. When 60 votes is functionally required for almost everything else, reconciliation stops being a niche budget tool and becomes the main highway. And every trip down that highway runs straight through one office that nobody elected.

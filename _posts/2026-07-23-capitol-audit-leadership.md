*Nine names run the House and Senate floors: the Speaker, the two chamber leaders, and the four whips who count votes for each party, plus their Senate counterparts. This audit holds all nine to one yardstick — floor attendance, bills personally sponsored that became law, the industries funding their campaigns, and anything on the public record — no matter which party they answer to. Reporting on documents, not legal advice.*

## The pattern

Republicans and Democrats land in almost the same place on this card — a 59 average for the five Republicans, a 67.5 average for the four Democrats — and that gap is explained entirely by attendance and bill counts, not by donors or scandal. The two cleanest records on paper, Thune and Clark, sit in different parties. The two most concentrated donor relationships, Barrasso's energy money and Schumer's Wall Street money, also sit in different parties. Nobody in either party is dirty by a wide margin, and nobody is clean by one either.

What the data does show clearly: donor concentration tracks committee assignment, not corruption in any provable sense. Barrasso is the nation's #1 recipient of coal and mining money and sits on energy and public-lands committees; Emmer built the House's crypto caucus and its biggest joint fundraising PAC pulled 80% of its money from a single pair of crypto executives. That correlation is worth printing. It is not, by itself, a crime.

## The scored card

The usual warning: **attendance percentages, bill counts, and donor dollar figures are measurements**, each sourced to GovTrack, Congress.gov, or OpenSecrets/FEC filings. **The final grade is not.** It is the journal's editorial blend of those measurements into a 0–100 judgment of the whole record: 100 for a leader with a strong record and no red flags, 0 for one actively compromised. We publish the inputs so you can argue with the math.

Scale: **85+** strong record, no red flags · **65–84** solid record, real but limited questions · **45–64** mixed — real conflicts alongside real output · **25–44** weak record, major concerns · **under 25** actively derelict or compromised.

A middling grade, and a fair one. Nobody on this card is clean of a large donor relationship, and nobody is buried by one either. The attendance gap between best (Clark and Thune/Schumer, under 1%) and worst (Scalise, 5.7%) is real, but so is the context behind the worst number on the list. This is a record of nine people doing a hard, compromised job with uneven amounts of both effort and entanglement — not a scandal sheet, and not a clean bill of health.

## The last word

Two findings matter more than the number. First: donor concentration in this dataset consistently maps onto committee assignment, not misconduct — Barrasso and energy, Emmer and crypto, Schumer and Wall Street, Johnson and Scalise and Gulf-state oil and gas. That is Congress working exactly as its committee system was designed to work, for better and worse; it is not evidence of a crime, and we don't print it as one. Second: not one of the nine carries an actual criminal charge or conviction. What exists instead is a handful of unadjudicated ethics complaints on both sides of the aisle — real filings, no findings, and we report them as exactly that.

*Ubi non est lex, non est transgressio* — where there is no law, there is no transgression. Grading Congress by an honest yardstick doesn't require finding a crime on every desk. It requires printing what's actually there, attributing it precisely, and letting the reader decide what a "conflict of interest" is worth at the ballot box.

*This is the pilot entry in a new series. Individual-member audits will expand from here; the institutional Senate and House scorecards — graded against Article I itself, the same way the Grievance Audit was graded against the Declaration — are next.*
